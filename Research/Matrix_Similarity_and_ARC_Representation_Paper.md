# Matrix Similarity, Puzzle Membership, and Input–Output Pair Representation in ARC-AGI-2: Evidence from Dimensionality and Line-Motif Analyses

**Author:** Motifs AI Research Laboratory  
**Date:** July 2026

---

## Abstract

A useful representation for ARC-AGI-2 must support three distinct, easily conflated decisions: whether two grids belong to the same puzzle, whether an input corresponds to a particular output within that puzzle, and which properties of an input–output pair constrain a candidate transformation. This paper synthesizes the repository’s feature-dimensionality and advanced same-color-line analyses to guide those decisions. The dimensionality experiment extracts 202 standardized, programmatic grid descriptors from 8,616 grids in 1,000 consolidated training tasks, then evaluates Euclidean matching after principal-component projections of 5–200 dimensions on a fixed random sample of 100 complete tasks. Puzzle matching rises rapidly from mean reciprocal rank (MRR) 0.698 at five dimensions to a maximum 0.815 at 40 dimensions (Top-1 0.758), then remains on a lower plateau near MRR 0.805. Input–output pairing peaks differently: 0.645 at 25 dimensions, with the reported 200-dimensional result 0.639. These results favor a compact geometry/topology representation for retrieval, rather than the premise that more coordinates necessarily improve it.

The line analysis supplies an orthogonal relational signal. It treats non-background horizontal and vertical runs of at least three cells as lines, while excluding runs contained in solid same-color rectangles. Of 1,000 tasks, 807 are line-dominant under the notebook’s mean-line-count criterion. Across line thresholds 3–9, within-puzzle presence remains 0.979–0.999, and input–output presence remains 0.686–0.872; each exceeds a cell-shuffled control. A 30-coordinate line embedding yields a sampled same-puzzle distance ROC-AUC of 0.766, and a Random Forest over input–output embedding differences reaches cross-validated ROC-AUC 0.940 ± 0.012 and accuracy 0.889 ± 0.013 under the notebook’s pair-level protocol. These are promising signals, not estimates of solver accuracy.

We recommend a hierarchical representation: compact connectivity, geometry, occupancy, transition, symmetry, and component-moment descriptors for broad retrieval; explicitly detected primitives, beginning with lines, for relational interpretation; and pair-difference features for transformation proposal and verification. The paper also identifies validation constraints—particularly task-grouped splits, fitting preprocessing only on training tasks, and avoiding candidate pools restricted to the true task—that must be repaired before claims of generalization are made.

## 1. Introduction

ARC-AGI-2 problems contain several levels of structure. A single grid has geometry, colors, components, adjacency patterns, symmetry, and spatial anchors. A puzzle is a small set of grids sharing a latent rule. An input–output pair is not merely a pair of similar images: it is evidence about an operator that may crop, recolor, copy, delete, align, count, or compose objects. A representation suitable for autonomous solving must preserve information at all three levels without mistaking a high score on one for competence at the others.

The two analyses examined here approach this need from complementary directions. `5.Feature_Dimensionality_Analysis.ipynb` asks how much of a broad, hand-programmed grid description should be retained for nearest-neighbor matching. `5.Advanced_Motif_Line_Analysis.ipynb` asks whether a narrow but semantically recognizable primitive—same-color line segments—recurs across a puzzle and survives input–output transitions. The former is a global descriptive representation; the latter is a primitive-specific relational representation. Their joint lesson is that a solver should not choose between topology and motifs. It should use topology to index and constrain a search, then use motif relations to formulate and test transformations.

This paper presents the empirical results before making recommendations. It distinguishes measured performance from inference, documents the available ablations, and treats the reported experiments as development evidence rather than a final benchmark. That distinction matters because the analyses operate over the consolidated training corpus and their evaluation candidates remain entangled with task identity in ways discussed below.

## 2. Data, representations, and evaluation procedures

Both primary notebooks load 8,616 matrices from 1,000 consolidated ARC training tasks. The dimensionality analysis applies four component segmentations: 4- and 8-neighborhood connectivity crossed with same-color and non-background grouping. Components smaller than three cells are omitted. Its 202 coordinates include twenty basic component statistics (count, mean and maximum size, and mean and maximum elongation in each segmentation), color diversity and border contact, six grid-geometry coordinates, ten absolute color proportions, count-ratio/difference and component-size statistics, border summaries, 45 unordered color-transition frequencies, five padded-grid symmetry scores, component bounding-box/centroid/fill/density moments, and 50 products or squares of selected base variables. Each coordinate is standardized across all 8,616 grids.

The notebook fits PCA to the full standardized feature matrix and evaluates projections from five to 200 coordinates in increments of five; nominal dimensions above 202 add very small random noise dimensions. It fixes a random seed and samples 100 complete tasks. Same-puzzle performance is based on Euclidean distances between all sampled grids: MRR is the reciprocal rank of the nearest grid from the same task, while Top-k is the proportion whose first same-task neighbor occurs within the first k neighbors. The input–output metric evaluates each training input against output grids *from the same task* and asks whether its nearest output has the matching pair index. Thus it is an in-task correspondence score, not a corpus-wide input-to-output retrieval score.

The line notebook detects maximal contiguous horizontal and vertical runs of a nonzero color with length at least three. A run is discarded when the complete span immediately above or below it has the same color; the symmetric left/right rule is used vertically. This removes runs embedded in solid rectangles, though it does not create a complete taxonomy of all filled or hollow figures. For a control, the notebook shuffles cells within each grid, preserving the grid dimensions and color histogram while disrupting spatial arrangement. Its 30-coordinate embedding comprises line counts and maximum lengths for colors 1–9, horizontal and vertical counts, corner/edge/interior counts, total count, mean and maximum length, line density, height, width, and aspect ratio. Same-puzzle ROC-AUC is computed from all within-task pairs in the first 200 tasks against one randomly drawn cross-task comparator per pair. For input–output classification, absolute differences of embeddings for the first 500 positive pairs and randomly selected outputs from different tasks are fed to a Random Forest with ordinary stratified five-fold cross-validation.

## 3. Results: dimensionality and feature contribution

The central dimensionality result is a rise followed by saturation, not a severe collapse. At five dimensions, MRR is 0.698, Top-1 is 0.614, and in-task input–output accuracy is 0.482. At 10 dimensions those quantities are 0.765, 0.694, and 0.506. The major gain is already visible by 20–25 dimensions: MRR 0.795/0.810, Top-1 0.731/0.750, and input–output accuracy 0.612/0.645, respectively. The best reported MRR is 0.815 at 40 dimensions, accompanied by the best Top-1 result of 0.758. The best input–output score is instead 0.645 at 25 dimensions. At 200 dimensions, MRR is 0.805, Top-1 0.748, and input–output accuracy 0.639. Consequently, 25–40 PCA coordinates are the practical retrieval region; there is no empirical basis here for selecting 100+ coordinates merely because they are available.

The available component ablation export helps identify what lies beneath that compact representation. Its 22-coordinate baseline obtains MRR 0.686, Top-1 0.594, and input–output accuracy 0.266. Size features alone are the strongest isolated family (MRR 0.497; Top-1 0.376; I/O 0.176), followed by elongation alone (0.378; 0.290; 0.138), count alone (0.231; 0.150; 0.060), and the two auxiliary descriptors alone (0.180; 0.090; 0.035). Necessity aligns with sufficiency: deleting sizes produces the largest baseline decline, to MRR 0.552 and I/O 0.191. Deleting elongation leaves MRR 0.594; deleting counts leaves 0.654; deleting auxiliary descriptors leaves 0.617. These figures establish component size as the primary topology signal, but show that every family contributes in combination.

Connectivity and grouping also matter. Eight-connectivity alone reaches MRR 0.673, nearly the full 22-coordinate baseline, whereas 4-connectivity alone reaches 0.592. Same-color-only descriptors score 0.627, compared with 0.502 for non-background-only descriptors. This makes a strong case for retaining 8-connected same-color component statistics as the core. It does not justify discarding 4-connectivity or non-background views: their difference captures diagonal joining and multicolor cohesion, two transformations ARC puzzles often exploit. The broad expansion evidence is similarly synergistic: the repository’s 52-coordinate comparison reports MRR 0.659 for connectivity-only, 0.786 for expanded-only, and 0.813 when combined. This supports combination, but the differing protocols mean these values should not be numerically compared with the PCA sweep.

PCA changes the interpretation of “individual predictive power.” A PCA coordinate is a linear mixture, so the sweep cannot say that any one raw color transition or centroid moment is individually predictive. The defensible conclusion is group-level: component size, 8-connectivity, and same-color segmentation are demonstrated contributors; geometry, occupancy, transitions, symmetry, and spatial moments are candidate supplements whose joint projection improves retrieval. A future raw-feature permutation or grouped leave-one-family-out experiment with task-held-out evaluation is required before ranking every expanded feature family.

## 4. Results: lines as a primitive-level signal

Lines are common but not universal. Among all matrices, the maximum-length histogram contains 2,424 matrices with no eligible line; among nonzero maxima the count is 1,281 at length three, 781 at four, 649 at five, 459 at six, and falls thereafter. This frequency pattern is consistent with short constructed runs being common and very long runs being constrained by grid size. The operational line-dominance threshold—mean at least one detected line per task—labels 807 of 1,000 tasks (80.7%) as line-dominant. The remaining 19.3% is substantial: a line representation must be a branch in a mixture of primitive experts, not a universal replacement for components.

Line occurrence is markedly stable at the puzzle level. Conditional on a grid containing a line of length at least N, another grid in the same puzzle contains one with probability 0.999, 0.996, 0.991, 0.989, 0.985, 0.985, and 0.979 for N=3 through 9. The corresponding shuffled-control probabilities range from 0.985 to 0.960. The absolute excess is modest for short lines because the histogram-preserving shuffle frequently creates them, but is consistently positive. Input-to-output conditional presence is more discriminating: 0.872, 0.808, 0.786, 0.759, 0.739, 0.710, and 0.686 versus shuffled rates 0.786, 0.743, 0.724, 0.688, 0.659, 0.636, and 0.615. Presence is therefore useful as a soft prior or candidate-rule constraint, not a proof that a particular line is copied.

Color and location provide richer relational evidence. Relative to shuffled controls, line color appears less in non-line cells (0.183 versus 0.355) and adjacent cells (0.290 versus 0.450). It is also lower under the notebook’s rate definition for other lines in the same matrix (0.616 versus 0.810), other puzzle lines (0.410 versus 0.508), and output lines (0.533 versus 0.695). The latter three observed values are lower, not higher, than their shuffled baselines. This corrects the notebook’s prose interpretation: the measured comparison does **not** support “higher color reuse than chance.” It does support spatial focality and contrast around an eligible line. Reuse across grids should be re-tested with a null that conditions on each grid’s color histogram and the number of detected lines, and with color identity treated separately from line prevalence.

Orientation results should receive the same discipline. Conditional on having lines, both horizontal and vertical orientations occur with probability 0.734 in real grids and 0.732 in shuffled grids, essentially no separation. Corner placement does separate (0.105 real versus 0.074 shuffled); edge placement is lower in real grids (0.335 versus 0.387); interior placement is higher (0.560 versus 0.539). The credible conclusion is corner anchoring, not a general boundary preference or unusual co-occurrence of orientations.

The learned line embedding has useful discriminatory power under its stated protocol: same-puzzle ROC-AUC is 0.766. The Random Forest pair-difference model reports I/O ROC-AUC 0.940 ± 0.012 and accuracy 0.889 ± 0.013. Finally, paired comparisons across the available I/O pairs show no reliable change in mean line count (6.645 input, 6.520 output; p=0.425) or maximum length (5.979, 5.899; p=0.328), but a reliable increase in corner-line ratio (0.117 to 0.163; paired t=-10.21, p=3.32×10^-24). Thus the most robust transformation-level signal is not line quantity but an increase in corner anchoring.

## 5. Discussion: representation choices and generalization beyond lines

The recommended base grid vector should retain the 8-connected same-color component count/size/elongation family, its 4-connected counterpart, the non-background counterpart, grid height/width/area/aspect, color occupancy, border contact, normalized color-transition frequencies, symmetry, and component-box moments. Component size is the priority because it is both sufficient and necessary in the available ablation. Eight-connected and same-color views are the priority configurations. The 25–40-dimensional PCA projection is an efficient retrieval index, but an explainable solver should retain the raw feature families too: projected coordinates cannot tell a program synthesizer which object, color, or anchor changed.

Expanded features should therefore be selected by role rather than appended indiscriminately. Geometry and occupancy predict canvas constraints and cropping. Component moments and fill ratios distinguish sparse arrangements, filled solids, and bounding boxes. Transition frequencies describe local color contact. Symmetries represent invariances. Cross-products are useful for distance matching but should be generated on demand or regularized, because they obscure causal interpretation and contribute to high-dimensional redundancy. Exact color IDs should be supplemented—not replaced—by color-permutation-invariant summaries, because ARC rules sometimes preserve semantic color roles and sometimes relabel colors.

The line result generalizes naturally through a primitive grammar. A square or rectangle detector should report filled versus hollow state, side lengths, thickness, interior color, border contact, symmetry axes, and containment. A hollow rectangle should additionally expose its opening status and whether objects lie inside, touch, or cross its boundary. Corners should be represented as ordered pairs of perpendicular rays with arm lengths, turn direction, color, and endpoint anchors. Crosses and Ts should be represented as a junction with degree, arm directions, arm-length vector, and whether the junction is centered or endpoint-attached. More complex figures should be decomposed into a graph of primitive strokes and regions: nodes are endpoints, corners, junctions, centers, and enclosed regions; edges are maximal runs or boundaries; attributes preserve color, thickness, length, and relative position. Canonicalization over rotation, reflection, translation, and optionally color permutation can then supply stable identities while retaining the transform that maps an instance to its canonical form.

This extrapolation must avoid the line detector’s specific blind spot. Because it removes runs contained in solid rectangles, it is intentionally not a detector for filled figures. A primitive system should permit overlapping explanations: a filled 3×5 rectangle is a region primitive, its perimeter is a boundary primitive, and its rows are not independently counted as semantic lines unless a task requires that view. Multiple parsings should be retained with costs rather than committing prematurely. This is crucial for ARC, where the same pixels can be interpreted as one object, several objects, a frame, or an occluding trace.

At puzzle level, representation should aggregate both invariants and controlled differences across demonstrations. Invariants include primitive inventory, relative orientation, repeated colors, counts, and symmetries. Differences include input-minus-output canvas size, objects added/deleted, color mappings, translations, rotations, component merges/splits, bounding-box crops, and changes in anchoring. A candidate transformation is credible only if it explains each training pair using the same parameterized rule. Matrix similarity is consequently a retrieval tool; puzzle belonging is evidence for a shared latent program; I/O pairing is a hypothesis test over transformations. These should have separate scores and separate candidate pools.

The path toward autonomous solving is a staged architecture. First, build a multi-view parse containing raw grids, components under both connectivities, primitive detections, and a scene graph. Second, compute a compact 25–40-dimensional index for training-example retrieval and a raw interpretable descriptor for diagnostics. Third, infer pair deltas in the scene graph and enumerate typed programs—crop, recolor, select-by-property, copy, move, reflect, rotate, extend, fill, draw boundary, and compose—conditioned on detected primitives. Fourth, use all training pairs to reject programs and predict output dimensions before rendering. Fifth, render the surviving program on the test input and check conservation, color, adjacency, and primitive constraints. A calibrated uncertainty mechanism should return multiple candidates where the training evidence underdetermines the rule.

## 6. Validity, limitations, and next experiments

The reported values are valuable development measurements but insufficient as held-out claims. PCA scaling and fitting use every grid before the 100-task evaluation subset is selected. The I/O nearest-output pool is restricted to the true task, eliminating the difficult first step of identifying the task or transformation family. The line I/O classifier uses ordinary pair-level folds, so examples from the same puzzle may enter both training and validation; negatives are deliberately from different tasks, a much easier contrast than wrong outputs from the same task. The shuffled controls are informative for spatial organization but do not establish statistical significance without repeated shuffles, uncertainty intervals, or a specified test. The notebooks also contain stale prose numbers that disagree with their displayed output, and several stated interpretations reverse the observed direction of a control comparison. This paper uses the displayed computations and CSV exports where they conflict.

The immediate experimental priority is a task-grouped benchmark. Split tasks, not grids or pairs, into development and test folds; fit standardization, PCA, feature selection, and learned classifiers only on development tasks. For I/O matching, evaluate both corpus-wide output retrieval and hard negatives drawn from other outputs within the same puzzle or transformation family. Report bootstrap confidence intervals, paired permutation tests for ablation changes, and chance-calibrated precision–recall as well as ROC-AUC. Run grouped removal, permutation, and conditional mutual-information ablations for geometry, color occupancy, transitions, symmetry, bounding-box moments, interactions, and each primitive family. Add a full rectangle/square/corner/cross/T detector and compare line-only, region-only, graph-only, and fused models on the same folds.

A final benchmark should be solver-facing. Measure not only retrieval and pair identification, but output-dimension accuracy, exact-grid accuracy, program consistency across demonstrations, and calibration of the candidate set. Include ablations that remove the retrieval index, primitive graph, pair-delta representation, and program verifier. Only then will similarity metrics be connected to autonomous ARC-AGI-2 solving rather than to descriptive association alone.

## 7. Conclusion

The evidence supports a layered, interpretable representation. A 25–40-dimensional projection of comprehensive topology and geometry is an effective compact index in the present experiment, while raw component size, 8-connectivity, and same-color grouping are the most defensible core features. Same-color lines add a high-coverage primitive signal and reveal a credible I/O trend toward corner anchoring, but they neither cover every task nor independently prove a transformation. Squares, rectangles, hollow frames, corners, junctions, crosses, Ts, and composite figures should extend the same primitive-and-relation vocabulary rather than compete with it. Progress toward autonomous ARC solving requires converting these observations into task-held-out, transformation-aware scene-graph program induction and verification.
